1. Cell Structure — animal & plant cells

Definition: A cell is the basic unit of life. All organisms are made of one or more cells.

Key organelles

  • Cell membrane: a thin layer that controls entry and exit of substances (selectively permeable)
  • Cytoplasm: gel-like fluid where chemical reactions occur
  • Nucleus: contains DNA and controls cell activities
  • Mitochondria: site of aerobic respiration — makes ATP
  • Ribosomes: where proteins are built
  • Cell wall (plants): rigid cellulose layer for support
  • Chloroplasts (plants): contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis
  • Vacuole (plants): stores cell sap and maintains turgor pressure

Annotated diagrams

(Simple diagrams are included for classroom use — draw from these descriptions)

  1. Animal cell: oval shape with membrane, nucleus, cytoplasm, mitochondria and ribosomes.
  2. Plant cell: rectangular outline with cell wall outside the membrane, large central vacuole, chloroplasts and smaller mitochondria.

Worked example

Q: Name two differences between plant and animal cells. A: Plant cells have chloroplasts and cell walls; animal cells do not. Plant cells often have a large central vacuole.

2. Cell Specialisation

Cells adapt their structure to perform specific functions. This process is called specialisation.

CellAdaptationFunction
SpermTail (flagellum), streamlined head, lots of mitochondriaSwim to the egg
NerveLong axon, branched endingsSend electrical signals over long distances
MuscleProtein fibres able to contract, many mitochondriaContract to produce movement
Root hairLong thin extension (hair)Increase surface area for water/mineral uptake
Palisade cellMany chloroplasts, tall shapeAbsorb light for photosynthesis

Exam tip

Always link structure to function — e.g. more mitochondria → more ATP produced → suits energy-demanding cells like muscle and sperm.

3. Types of Cells: Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic

Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) have no nucleus, small size (0.1–5 μm), and sometimes have plasmids.

Eukaryotic cells (plants, animals, fungi) have a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles and are larger (10–100 μm).

Example comparison

  • DNA: free in cytoplasm (prokaryote) vs inside nucleus (eukaryote)
  • Organelles: no chloroplasts in most bacteria, but in plant eukaryotes yes
4. Standard Form & Units

Cells and organelles are tiny. Use micrometres (μm) and standard form for accuracy.

  • 1 mm = 1000 μm
  • 1 μm = 1000 nm
  • Common conversions: 0.02 mm = 20 μm; 3 μm = 3 × 10⁻⁶ m

5. Microscopy

Microscopes magnify small objects — two common types below.

  • Light microscope: beams of light, up to ~2000× (typical school microscopes 40–400×). Good for cells and tissues.
  • Electron microscope: electrons, much higher magnification and resolution — can see organelle details. Not used in normal school practicals.

Magnification formula

magnification = image size ÷ actual size

Worked example: if image is 40 mm and actual cell is 0.04 mm, magnification = 40 / 0.04 = 1000×.

6. Modes of Transport

Diffusion

Net movement of particles from high concentration → low concentration until equilibrium. Does not require energy.

Example: Oxygen diffusing from alveoli into blood.

Osmosis

Special case of diffusion: movement of water through a partially permeable membrane from high water potential to low water potential.

Example: Plant root cells gaining water from soil.

Active transport

Movement of particles against a concentration gradient, using energy (ATP).

Example: Mineral ions absorbed into root hair cells from dilute soil.

Factors affecting diffusion/osmosis

  • Concentration gradient
  • Surface area
  • Temperature
  • Distance (thinner exchange surfaces are faster)
7. Microscopy Required Practical (RP)

Aim

To prepare and observe plant and animal cells using a light microscope.

Method (step-by-step)

  1. Place a drop of water on a clean slide.
  2. Using forceps, peel a thin layer of onion epidermis (or use a cheek swab for animal cells).
  3. Place the sample on the slide and add a drop of iodine (staining improves contrast).
  4. Gently place a coverslip at an angle to avoid air bubbles.
  5. Start with low-power objective, focus, then move to higher magnification.
  6. Draw observed cells and label organelles.

Safety

Handle blades and glassware carefully. Dispose of biological samples hygienically and wash hands.

What to record

  • Magnification used
  • Cell features observed (nucleus, cell wall, chloroplasts, vacuole, etc.)
  • Annotated drawings
8. Osmosis Required Practical (RP)

Aim

To investigate the effect of different sugar solution concentrations on potato cylinder mass (evidence for osmosis).

Method

  1. Cut identical potato cylinders (same length and diameter) and record initial masses.
  2. Place cylinders into labelled beakers of solutions (e.g. 0%, 10%, 20%, 30% sugar).
  3. Leave for set time (30–60 minutes), remove, blot dry, weigh again.
  4. Calculate percentage mass change: ((final - initial)/initial) × 100%

Expected results

In pure water (0%) the potato will gain mass (water enters) — positive change. In concentrated sugar, potato will lose mass (water leaves) — negative change.

Variables

  • Independent: solution concentration
  • Dependent: mass change
  • Control: temperature, time, size of potato cylinders

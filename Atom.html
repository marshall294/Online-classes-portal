1. Atoms — structure and particles

Atom: smallest part of an element that retains chemical identity.

Subatomic particles

  • Proton (p⁺): +1 charge, relative mass ≈ 1, located in nucleus.
  • Neutron (n⁰): 0 charge, relative mass ≈ 1, located in nucleus.
  • Electron (e⁻): −1 charge, negligible mass, orbit in shells.

Notation

Atomic number (Z) = number of protons. Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons.

Example notation: ²³Na (mass number 23) or standard form Na (atomic number 11).

Worked example

Chlorine-35 (³⁵Cl): Z = 17 → 17 protons, electrons = 17 (neutral), neutrons = 35−17 = 18.

2. Elements — symbols and periodic shorthand

An element contains only one type of atom. Each element has a symbol, usually 1 or 2 letters. First letter capitalised, second lowercase.

Common examples: H, He, Li, Be, B, C, N, O, F, Ne, Na, Mg, Al, Si, P, S, Cl, Ar, K, Ca.

Examples with properties

  • H (Hydrogen) — atomic number 1, gas at room temp.
  • O (Oxygen) — atomic number 8, essential for respiration.
  • Na (Sodium) — reactive metal, reacts with water.
  • Cl (Chlorine) — reactive non-metal, forms NaCl.
3. Compounds — bonding and formulas

Compound: substance made when elements chemically combine in fixed ratios. Shown by a chemical formula.

Types of bonding (basic)

  • Ionic bonding: transfer of electrons (metal + non-metal). Example: NaCl (Na⁺ Cl⁻).
  • Covalent bonding: sharing of electrons (non-metal + non-metal). Example: H₂O, CO₂.

Worked examples

Water, H₂O: 2 hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to 1 oxygen.

Sodium chloride: Na (11 → 2,8,1) loses 1 electron → Na⁺; Cl (17 → 2,8,7) gains 1 electron → Cl⁻; ionic bond forms.

4. Separating Techniques — mixtures (not chemical compounds)

Separating techniques are used for mixtures where substances are physically mixed not chemically bonded.

List & brief use

  • Filtration: separates insoluble solids from liquids (sand + water).
  • Evaporation: removes solvent to leave dissolved solids (salt from saltwater).
  • Simple distillation: separate liquids with different boiling points (pure water from salt solution).
  • Fractional distillation: separate liquids with closer boiling points (fractions from crude oil).
  • Chromatography: separates substances based on solubility/affinity (separating dyes).

Practical example

Separating a mixture of sand and salt: dissolve mixture in water (salt dissolves), filter to remove sand, evaporate water to recover salt.

5. Electron configuration — shells & patterns

Electrons fill shells around the nucleus. A simple school model uses capacities 2, 8, 8 (first three shells).

Examples

  • H: 1 → 1
  • He: 2 → 2
  • Li: 3 → 2,1
  • Be: 4 → 2,2
  • O: 8 → 2,6
  • Na: 11 → 2,8,1
  • Cl: 17 → 2,8,7

Why it matters

The number of outer (valence) electrons determines chemical reactivity and which ions an atom forms.

6. Ion formation — cations & anions

Atoms become ions to achieve a more stable electron configuration (usually a full outer shell like a noble gas).

Examples

  • Sodium: Na → loses 1 e⁻ → Na⁺ (2,8)
  • Chlorine: Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (2,8,8)
  • Magnesium: Mg → loses 2 e⁻ → Mg²⁺

Ionic formulae

Combine charges to make neutral compounds: e.g., Mg²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → MgCl₂

7. Development of the Periodic Table — history & modern layout

Key stages in understanding elements and their arrangement.

  1. Dobereiner (1829): triads (groups of three with similar properties).
  2. Newlands (1864): Law of Octaves — properties repeat every 8 elements (not perfect).
  3. Mendeleev (1869): arranged elements by atomic mass and left gaps for predicted elements; could predict properties of undiscovered elements.
  4. Modern periodic table (1913+): arranged by atomic number (Z) after discovery of proton and electronic structure — explains periodicity.

Groups & periods

Vertical columns = groups (same valence electrons). Horizontal rows = periods (same number of electron shells).

Examples

  • Group 1: Li, Na, K — 1 valence electron, very reactive metals.
  • Group 7 (17): F, Cl, Br — halogens, reactive non-metals.
  • Group 0 (18): He, Ne, Ar — noble gases, inert due to full outer shells.
8. Common practicals & demonstration ideas

Chromatography of inks

Use paper chromatography to separate dyes in pens. Observe Rf values and compare pigments.

Flame tests

Sodium gives yellow flame, lithium red, potassium lilac, copper green/blue. (Follow safety rules — small samples, Bunsen flame, goggles.)

Simple distillation

Distill water from a salt solution to demonstrate separation by boiling point.
